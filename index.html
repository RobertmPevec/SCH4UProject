Powering the Future:
Lithium-Ion Batteries and Renewable Energy

By: Robert Pevec

Overview:

Lithium-ion batteries are a compact and widely used type of rechargeable energy storage device. They derive their energy from a chemical reaction that occurs within the battery's components, primarily involving lithium compounds and other materials. Lithium batteries are known for their exceptional energy density, which surpasses that of many other battery types. This high energy density makes them a popular choice for various portable and electronic devices, electric vehicles and renewable energy systems.

Inquiry Questions:

What Is the Chemistry behind Lithium-Ion Batteries? What Makes Them Rechargeable?

Lithium-ion batteries function through an electrochemical process involving three main components: the anode, the cathode and an electrolyte.

The anode is typically made of graphite and releases lithium ions during discharge.

The cathode is comprised of lithium-containing compounds such as lithium cobalt oxide and combines with lithium ions during discharge to release energy.

An electrolyte is a lithium salt-based solution that facilitates the movement of lithium ions between the anode and cathode.

The reason lithium-ion batteries are rechargeable is because the chemical reactions at the anode and cathode are reversible. While charging, an external voltage drives lithium ions back to the anode, and this process can be repeated, allowing the battery to be charged and discharged multiple times (Let's Talk Science, 2019).

Is This Type of Battery Safe to Use? How Long Can They Be Used?

Lithium-ion batteries are generally safe when used correctly but pose risks if mishandled or damaged and can overheat or even catch fire under extreme conditions.

Battery lifespan depends on usage patterns and environmental factors although they can typically endure hundreds to thousands of charge cycles over their lifetime. Lithium-ion batteries experience capacity degradation over time due to chemical changes that occur within the battery. Each charge-discharge cycle causes microscopic structural alterations in the battery's electrodes, which reduce their ability to store and release energy. This is the result of the repeated movement of lithium ions between the anode and cathode. As the battery's capacity diminishes, the battery holds less charge, resulting in shorter runtime between charges (Battery University, 2022).

Chemical Reactions

Oxidation Half-Reaction (Anode):
Li(s) → Li+(aq) + e-
Reduction Half-Reaction (Cathode):
LiCoO2 (s) + e- → Li+(aq) + CoO2 (s)
Overall Discharge Reaction:
Li(s) + LiCoO2 (s) → 2Li+(aq) + CoO2 (s)

Chemical Reaction When Lithium-ion Batteries Corrode:
LiPF6 → LiF + PF5
PF5 + H2O → POF3 + 2HF

Will Lithium-ion Batteries Corrode/Leak?

Lithium-ion batteries themselves do not corrode in the same way that metals like iron or steel do when exposed to moisture and oxygen. Lithium-ion batteries are sealed and designed to contain their internal components, including the electrolyte, which is a liquid or gel-like substance. However, like any technology, they are not completely immune to failure, and under certain conditions, leakage or corrosion can occur. For lithium-ion batteries, HF is produced due to the reaction of moisture with the electrolyte. HF is a particularly corrosive acid, which can cause serious damage to aluminum-plastic films, fluid collectors, and cathode materials (A similar chemical reaction is listed to the left). The following are some factors that will contribute to corrosion or leakage in a lithium-ion battery:

(Paleblue, 2022)
  • Physical Damage: Physical damage to the battery, such as punctures, dents, or impacts, can compromise the integrity of the battery's casing. This damage can lead to electrolyte leakage or even short circuits.
  • Overheating: Overcharging, short-circuiting or the presence of external heat can cause this thermal runaway. The uncontrolled increase in temperature and pressure within the battery results in degradation of the electrolyte which can leak (liquid or gas), ignite and explode (Fdedee, 2023).
  • Age/Wear: Degradation is a natural process and can be accelerated by factors like frequent deep discharges and high-temperature exposure.
  • Manufacturing Defects: manufacturing defects or flaws in the battery design can lead to leakage or other problems. This is why quality control and safety standards are critical in battery production (Dnk Power, 2023).

The Impacts of Lithium-ion Batteries on Society, the Environment and Health and Safety:

As we enter into a new era with a commitment to achieving zero emissions and seeking an alternative to fossil fuels, lithium-ion batteries have emerged as a pivotal player in our pursuit of sustainable energy solutions. While these batteries hold the promise of a greener future, it's essential to acknowledge their potential downsides. This includes concerns surrounding the extraction of materials like cobalt or lithium, which not only present environmental challenges but also underscore the profoundly troubling labor conditions endured by those involved in their extraction.

What Are the Environmental Impacts of Lithium-Ion Batteries?

The mining of lithium, cobalt, and other raw materials for battery production can harm ecosystems and result in habitat destruction. In some regions, mining activities have led to water contamination, soil degradation and greenhouse emissions due to the manufacturing processes to create these batteries.

The transportation of components for lithium-ion batteries is another significant environmental concern. While these batteries offer a greener alternative during use by reducing greenhouse gas emissions, their supply chain activities can partially negate these advantages. The long-distance movement of raw materials and battery components can contribute to carbon emissions and air pollution. According to the Argonne National Laboratory model, for a Tesla Model 3 to become more environmentally friendly than a gas-powered Toyota Corolla, it would require driving approximately 13,500 miles (21,725 km). This implies that, over the typical 12-year lifespan of a vehicle, an electric vehicle option would emit notably fewer emissions compared to the average gasoline-powered vehicle although is still not fully free from supply chain emissions (Reuters, 2021).

What are the Societal Impacts Involved with the Making of Lithium-ion Batteries?

While lithium-ion batteries have unquestionably improved our lives, it's essential to examine the societal impacts associated with their production. One of the most pressing concerns comes from the labor involved with the extraction of vital raw materials like cobalt and lithium. According to an article by Relevant a crucial component in rechargeable batteries found in smartphones, computers, and electric vehicles, is often mined in slave-like conditions in the Democratic Republic of Congo. This issue underscores the moral obligation of industries and companies to ensure that the materials used in lithium-ion batteries are sourced responsibly, respecting human rights and promoting equitable labor practices (Relevant, 2023).

Conclusion:

In conclusion, lithium-ion batteries have revolutionized energy storage by offering exceptional longevity and reusability. However, although they play a crucial role in our lives, they also may pose safety and environmental challenges. Mishandling and degradation can lead to safety risks, while raw material mining and supply chain emissions impact the environment. Moreover, there are also extremely serious societal concerns arising from labor conditions from material extraction, particularly in regions like the Democratic Republic of Congo. So as we embrace these batteries in our quest for sustainable energy, it's essential to balance their advantages with these challenges because achieving this balance ensures that lithium-ion batteries contribute to a cleaner, more sustainable world.
